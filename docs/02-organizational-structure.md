# Organizational Structure

## Overview

The Forge Framework defines a flexible organizational structure that scales from a single person to a multi-department company. The key insight is that **departments are not a fixed list** — they are whatever domains you need to organize. The framework provides the consistent *internal structure* for each domain; you decide what the domains are based on your context.

## The Core Principle

> Every domain of responsibility — whether it's a corporate department, a life area, or a creative pursuit — benefits from the same internal structure: Action Logs, Functional Folders, Process Folders, and Control Documents.

The "four departments" is a **recommended starting template**, not a rigid rule. The number and names of departments change based on what you're organizing.

## The Organization Model

```
Organization (or Life System)
├── Domain (What is being organized)
├── Dimension (Scale and scope)
└── Departments / Forges (The domains of responsibility)
    ├── [Domain 1]  ──┐
    ├── [Domain 2]    │  Each uses identical
    ├── [Domain 3]    │  internal structure
    └── [Domain 4]  ──┘
```

## Template: SaaS Business Startup

**Recommended for:** Companies, especially SaaS startups and small businesses.

| Department | Purpose | Sub-functions |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| **Administration** | Governance and support backbone | Compliance, Finance, HR, Security |
| **Clientele** | External-facing operations | Client Relationships, Sales/BD, Media/Marketing, Vendor Management |
| **Product** | Core value-creation engine | Development, Operations, Quality, Innovation |
| **Resources** | Operational support infrastructure | Technology, Knowledge Management, Procurement, Facilities |

### Administration
Keep the organization legal, funded, staffed, and secure.
- **Compliance** — Regulatory adherence, standards, audits
- **Finance** — Budget, accounting, financial reporting
- **HR** — People, hiring, culture, employee management
- **Security** — Information security, access control, incident response

### Clientele
Manage all relationships and interactions outside the organization.
- **Client Relationships** — Account management, client communications
- **Sales & Business Development** — Revenue generation
- **Media & Marketing** — Brand, communications, public presence
- **Vendor Actions** — External vendor/partner management

### Product
Create and deliver the organization's core value proposition.
- **Development** — Building products, services, or solutions
- **Operations** — Running and maintaining what's been built
- **Quality** — Standards, testing, continuous improvement
- **Innovation** — Research, new capabilities, future direction

### Resources
Provide the tools, knowledge, and infrastructure the organization needs.
- **Technology** — IT, tools, platforms, infrastructure
- **Knowledge Management** — Documentation, training, institutional knowledge
- **Procurement** — Acquiring what the organization needs
- **Facilities** — Physical or virtual workspace management

## Designing Your Own Departments

The framework doesn't prescribe *what* your domains are — it prescribes *how* each domain is structured internally. When designing your departments, consider:

1. **What are the major domains of responsibility?** — What areas of your life or organization need deliberate management?
2. **Are they distinct enough?** — Each department should have a clear boundary. If two domains constantly overlap, they might be one.
3. **Do they cover the full scope?** — Together, your departments should cover everything you're responsible for. No aspect of life is left out.
4. **Can each sustain the structure?** — Each department gets Action Logs, Functional Folders, etc. Is there enough activity in each domain to warrant the structure?

## Department Internal Structure

Regardless of what a department is called or what domain it covers, the internal pattern is always the same:

```
Department / Forge
├── Control Document (Department-level governance)
├── Action Log (Central task tracking)
├── Functional Folders
│   ├── Queue (Active work)
│   ├── Projects (Complex, ongoing work)
│   ├── Binder (Reference material)
│   └── Archive (Completed/historical work)
└── Process Folders
    ├── Process (Defined procedures)
    ├── Process in Play (Active procedures being executed)
    ├── Tracker (Monitoring instruments)
    └── Tracker-Log (Historical tracking data)
```

*See [03-functional-folders.md](03-functional-folders.md) for detailed explanation of the folder system.*

## Naming Conventions

In practice, departments are prefixed with an organization identifier:

| Context | Prefix | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Fourth State Communications | `4S` | `4S.A: Administration` |
| Kelvin Education | `KE` | `KE.C: Clientele` |
| Keyes Forge (Personal umbrella) | `KF` | `KF.R: RaihnForge` |
| RaihnForge | `KF.R` | `KF.R - Action Log` |
| UnchosenForge | `KF.UF` | `KF.UF - Action Log` |

For businesses, department codes are typically single letters:
- `A` = Administration, `C` = Clientele, `P` = Product, `R` = Resources

For personal Forges, the Forge name itself serves as the identifier.

## Operational Branches

Within the organization model, each department is a **branch of business** (or branch of life). Each branch:

- Has its own **functional folders** (identical structure)
- Generates its own **projects** (complex actions/items/processes)
- Maintains its own **action log** for tracking
- Feeds into the **organizational control** document at the top level

The top-level **Control** document serves as the operations "bible" — linking all indexes across departments/forges into a single reference point.

## Scaling the Structure

### Solo Operator / Personal Life
A single person runs all forges/departments themselves. The structure still applies — it just means one person managing all domains. The value is that life/work is organized by *function and purpose*, not by time or random filing.

### Small Team (2-10)
People begin to own specific departments. The shared structure means everyone knows where everything lives.

### Growing Company (10-50+)
Departments become actual teams. Consistent structure means onboarding is predictable — every new hire learns the same folder pattern regardless of department.

### Enterprise
Departments may subdivide further, but the department model and functional folder pattern remain consistent at every level.
